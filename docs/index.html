Mad Max F1Tenth

Using sensors and controllers to autonomously drive an F1Tenth car around the track as fast and safely as possible.

1University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, 2ECE 484 - Principles of Safe Autonomy

Abstract

This project develops an autonomous control system for an F1Tenth race car to complete a three-lap time trial as quickly and reliably as possible. The primary challenge is enabling the vehicle to navigate a track at high speed while avoiding collisions using only onboard sensors such as LiDAR, camera, and IMU. Because penalties are applied for crashes and rescues, the system must balance speed, stability, and safety.

Our approach focuses on building a modular autonomous driving pipeline consisting of perception, localization, path planning, and control. LiDAR data is used to detect track boundaries and obstacles, allowing the system to estimate the drivable corridor. From this information, a path planning module determines an optimal trajectory along the track center while accounting for curvature and available space. A pure pursuit controller then computes steering commands based on a lookahead point along the planned path, while a speed controller adjusts velocity depending on track conditions and turning radius.

The system is developed and tested within the F1Tenth simulator before deployment on the physical vehicle. Through simulation experiments and iterative tuning, we aim to achieve fast lap times while maintaining robust performance under varying track conditions. The final goal is a reliable autonomous racing system capable of completing the course with minimal penalties and competitive completion times.

System Approach

Our system will be built as a simple pipeline that takes sensor data from the car, understands the track around it, plans a path to follow, and then controls the vehicle to stay on that path.

Perception:
- We use sensor data from the LiDAR and camera to detect the track boundaries and nearby obstacles. This lets us estimate the drivable region of the track at any given moment.

Localization:
- Using simulator feedback and information from the perception module, the car estimates its position and orientation relative to the track.

Path Planning:
- From the detected track boundaries, we generate a centerline waypoint path that the car should follow. This path keeps the vehicle safely within the lane while still allowing it to move quickly through turns.

Control:
- To follow the path, we use a pure pursuit controller that steers the vehicle toward a lookahead point along the trajectory. The car’s speed is adjusted based on how sharp the upcoming turns are to maintain stability.

Sensor Evaluation

One of our goals is to determine whether LiDAR or camera data is more reliable for detecting track limits.

With LiDAR, we will use distance measurements to detect walls and estimate the track corridor from the scan data.

With the camera, we will experiment with image-based methods to detect lane boundaries or track edges.

We will compare the two approaches based on:

  • How accurately they detect the track limits
  • How stable the detection is while the car is moving
  • How sensitive they are to noise or lighting changes
  • How efficiently they run in real time

This will help us decide whether our final system should rely mainly on LiDAR, camera perception, or a combination of both.
